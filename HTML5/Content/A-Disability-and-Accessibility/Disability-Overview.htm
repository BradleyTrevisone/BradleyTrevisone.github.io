An Overview of Disability and Accessibility

An Overview of Disability

Our understanding of disability has developed significantly: from the term's origins, to the Americans with Disabilities Act in the 1990s, and continues to grow as the new frontier of the internet and web-spaces brings a new dimension to our daily lives.

To begin, it's important to consider how our understanding of disabilities has developed and how it affects more adults than would be assumed.

Medical Model Vs. Social Model

Historically, disability is understood through the medical model which addresses the disability as a personal problem. For example, if a person is not able to use a staircase, the medical model approach to this problem is to attempt adapting the body (or the mind) of the disabled person to the world as it exists. One solution could be creating a wheelchair capable of climbing stairs for the individual, but this creates a new term called "disability dongles" which refers to these created items that aim to return the disabled person to normalcy, or for this example able to climb stairs. These items are typically targeted for only a specific disabled audience and they are impractical for daily use due to bulk, instability, uncomfortable, and expensive for an overly designed object, that typically does not involve a disabled person in the creation process.

In a modernized perspective, the social model shifts the focus off the individual and onto the world surrounding the individual. It suggests that the individual is not disabled by their body or by their mind, but its the failed accommodations in design or in society that disable the individual.

To continue the staircase example, the lack of ramps or lifts limits the access of the building opposed to the wheelchair. It works similarly with communication disabilities as the individual is only excluded after there are no other means of communication available, like sign language or writing.

Disability's Impact on Everybody

Disability across The United States is far more common than one might think, affecting 28.7% of the population.

The Adobe Inclusive Design Workshop discusses the different types of disabilities. It emphasizes the importance of understanding that "many disabilities are invisible to observers" and can exist in three categories.

Category Definition Description
Sensory Ability to see, hear, or feel.

  • Sensory disabilities occur in degrees, such as visual impairment that can be corrected to 20/20 vision or an individual not being totally blind when they have partial vision.
Physical Affects a person’s mobility, dexterity, or stamina.

  • A gross motor disability affects someone’s ability to control large muscle groups, like ones that control the arms or legs

  • A fine motor disability affects the ability to control smaller muscle groups, like the ones found in the hand

  • Also includes paralysis, amputation, deformity.
Intellectual, Developmental, Neurological, and Psychological

  • Intellectual: Cognitive capacity and adaptability

  • Developmental: relating to ability to acquire and contextualize knowledge over a lifetime

  • Neurological: relating to the nervous system

  • Psychological: relating to emotional well being

While these categories are distinct, there is a high comorbidity rate between them.

However, disability in the terms of accessibility can affect individuals for any amount of time between a life time or even the within minutes like when opening a door while carrying groceries. This approach towards disability is greatly beneficial for the larger society and is referred to as universal design.
