An Overview of Disability and Accessibility

An Overview of Disability

Our understanding of disability has developed significantly: from the term's origins, to the Americans with Disabilities Act in the 1990s, and continues to grow as the new frontier of the internet and web-spaces brings a new dimension to our daily lives.

To begin, it's important to consider how our understanding of disabilities has developed and how it affects more adults than would be assumed.

Medical Model Vs. Social Model

Historically, disability is understood through the medical model which addresses the disability as something is wrong with an individuals body or their mind (for neurological disabilities) rather than the environment. For example, A person in a wheelchair wants to enter a building and the entrance is at the top of come across a set of stairs but there is not a ramp for them to use as an alternative. For a solution to this disability, the medical model would view the problem with the entrance by centering the inaccessibility around the disabled person with an individual need instead of the accessibility problem being the design oversight for the entrance itself. This second approach is the social model.

A modernized perspective of disability is the social model, which considers how it the environment itself is disabling the individual. Continuing the staircase example, the disability occurs after person in the wheelchair faces exclusion after reaching a building without an accessible entrance. Among all disabilities, accessibility issues are not always as well known as wheelchair ramps are; learning more about all the accessibility requirements and implementing them as early as possible into the designing phase. While this results in a better overall final product, it has potential to create unique and creative development solutions.

Faults of Bad Accessibility

When accessibility into the design process too late or after publication, the implementing can be less functional and appears as a slap-on edition. Bellow are two photo examples of ramps that show the difference between accessibility being implemented early in the design process for a seamlessly versus the clunky post production addition.

Another practice that promotes bad accessibility happens when designers or creators build a product without working directly with people with disabilities or the target user audience. This appears is through "evolutionary products", for example smart glasses that enforce eye contact for autistic children, and fall under the category of disability dongles. These innovations use the medical model and attempt to fix the body or mind of the disabled individual but are usually too impractical for daily use due to bulk, instability, uncomfortable, and expensive; but they may end up discarded because the creation is not wanted by the user.

Its important to include accessibility into the design of a product, learn about your users and what their individual needs are, and be receptive to accessibility feedback.

Disability's Impact on Everybody

Disability across The United States is far more common than one might think, affecting 28.7% of the population, so the learning about different disabilities and how it affects individual applies to far more people rather than viewing disabled people as niche users.

  • 13.9 percent of U.S. adults have a cognition disability with serious difficulty concentrating, remembering, or making decisions.

  • 12.2 percent of U.S. adults have a mobility disability with serious difficulty walking or climbing stairs.

  • 7.7 percent of U.S. adults have an independent living disability with difficulty doing errands alone.

  • 6.2 percent of U.S. adults are deaf or have serious difficulty hearing

  • 5.5 percent of U.S. adults have a vision disability with blindness or serious difficulty seeing even when wearing glasses.

  • 3.6 percent of U.S. adults have a self-care disability with difficulty dressing or bathing.

 The Information is provided by U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and more information can be found in their page Disability Impacts All of Us.

These disabilities are categorized into three categories in the Adobe Inclusive Design Workshop of sensory, physical, and mental, combining four sub categories of intellectual, developmental, neurological, and psychological disabilities. An important aspect of disability mentioned in this book is "many disabilities are invisible to observers," and this emphasizes the importance of designing with empathy and inclusion in mind, even when needs aren't immediately visible.

Category Definition Description
Sensory Ability to see, hear, or feel.

  • Sensory disabilities occur in degrees, such as visual impairment that can be corrected to 20/20 vision or an individual not being totally blind when they have partial vision.
Physical Affects a person’s mobility, dexterity, or stamina.

  • A gross motor disability affects someone’s ability to control large muscle groups, like ones that control the arms or legs

  • A fine motor disability affects the ability to control smaller muscle groups, like the ones found in the hand

  • Also includes paralysis, amputation, deformity.
Intellectual, Developmental, Neurological, and Psychological

  • Intellectual: Cognitive capacity and adaptability

  • Developmental: relating to ability to acquire and contextualize knowledge over a lifetime

  • Neurological: relating to the nervous system

  • Psychological: relating to emotional well being

While these categories are distinct, there is a high comorbidity rate between them.
