Accessibility and Universal Design

Creating accessible content is beneficial for all intended users beyond the disability association with accessibility, also referred to as universal design. It's important to keep these guidelines and requirements in mind during the design process, ideally from the earliest stages of development, because its much easier to create with accessibility in mind versus having to adjust everything in the end.

Universal Design in Everyday Items

Without context, understanding how to apply accessibility into design may seem overwhelming for everything that could be considered or too unknown that it doesn't get implemented at all. This is simplified with universal design, which, intends to make products usable by all people, to the greatest extent possible, without further adaptation or special designs, and this requires having accessibility in mind during early design stages.

There are everyday items that began as tools for specific tasks that a disabled person struggled with, for example the typewriter, which was created because an individual couldn't write by hand due to her blindness (Hoyt, A. 2024). Other common examples can be found Howstuffworks' article, 8 Everyday Items Originally Invented for People With Disabilities but doorknobs are an excellent example to discuss.

It goes to show how accessibility can be a simple alternative and benefit everybody. By switching a door knob to a door handle, the door can now be opened by somebody carrying in groceries or a disabled person that struggles with gripping or twisting motions equally. (Kalbag, Accessibility for everyone)

Incorporating Accessibility into Digital Spaces

When designing digital content, it's important to keep universal design in mind from the earliest stages of development. There is a natural lack of knowledge on accessibility, that's because most people are not constantly thinking about what others may need and most features are hidden from users. Thankfully, there is an extensive online resource that provides guidelines that comply with the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).

What is WCAG

WCAG stands for Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, and they are a set of guidelines developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) to provide technical standards to make the digital world accessible to people with disabilities.

These guideline standards is composed of four main elements: perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust, often called POUR.(Rivenburgh, K. 2025)

  • Perceivable: Information must be perceivable to people using only one of their senses, so they understand all related content.

  • Operable: End users must be able to interact with all webpage elements.

  • Understandable: end users must be able to understand web page content and functionality information.

  • Robust: Your website must effectively communicate information to all users, including users of assistive technologies, and remain compatible with evolving technologies and user needs.

Assistive Technology

The table below lists common assistive technologies with their function that must be accounted for in the programs code as well as be compatible with software and hardware.

Assistive Technology Purpose / Functionality Designing Tips
Screen Magnifiers Adjust text or font size, spacing, color, and sync with speech to improve readability Do not used fixed font sizes in CSS
Screen Readers Read text aloud using synthesized speech or convert it to braille Use HTML semantic elements
Text-to-Speech Software Converts written text into spoken words Avoid pictures of text
Speech Recognition Software Allows control of a computer through spoken commands Ensure all interactive elements are keyboard navigable
Alternative Keyboards Simulate keyboard input via head pointers, sip/puff, switches, controllers, etc. Ensure the whole website is navigable with the tab key
Alternative Pointing Devices Simulate mouse movement and clicking functions Use large clickable buttons and links

The information is sourced from W3C from the Assistive Technologies section of Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).

 

Interface Design

Interface design focuses on creating layouts, navigation, and interactions that are usable by everyone beyond the general accessibility guidelines. As an example, the headache of trying navigate a website on a smart phone but its the same scale as it appears on a computer screen. The creator hasn't considered scaleability for different viewports or if an individual needs to zoom in on the page to read the content. It immediately expose the lack of accessibility and universal design, and oversights like these are glaring issues when they are not considered or implemented correctly.

The Image and table bellow are tips by W3C to meet the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) requirements and can generally enhance your designs by incorporating more Universal Design strategies and accessibility features to be aware of in the early stages of development.

More information can be found in the article Designing for Web Accessibility, in order to meet Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) requirements.

Guideline Requirements Guideline Requirements
Contrast

  • Foreground text must have sufficient contrast with background colors

  • Applies to text on images, gradients, buttons, and other elements

  • Does not apply to logos or incidental text (e.g. text in photos)

 Scalable Designs

  • Consider how content displays in different viewport sizes

  • Repositioning main elements like headers and navigation

  • Revise text size and line width for readability and legibility
Dont Rely on Color Alone

  • Color can help convey information but shouldn't be the only method

  • Always include a non-color cue to differentiate elements

 Image and Media Alternatives

  • Visible links to transcripts of audio

  • Visible links to audio described versions of videos

  • Text along with icons and graphical buttons

  • Captions and descriptions for tables or complex graphs
Easy Navigation

  • Keep navigation naming, styling, and positioning consistent across pages

  • Offer multiple navigation methods (e.g. search, site map)

  • Provide orientation cues to show users where they are on the site

 Controls for Auto-Play

  • Provide visible controls to stop animations or auto-playing sound

  • Use for: image sliders, background sound, videos, and carousels.

Identifiable Elements

  • Use distinct styles for interactive elements like links and buttons

  • Make interactive elements easy to identify and interactivity through visual cues

  • Apply styles and naming consistently across the website

 Label Form Elements

  • Provide a descriptive label for each form field

  • Place labels to the left or above the field (left-to-right languages)

  • For checkboxes and radio buttons, position labels to the right

  • Avoid having too much space between labels and fields
Group Related Content

  • Use whitespace and proximity to show relationships between content

  • Style headings to group related content

  • Reduce visual clutter Make content easier to scan and understand

 Identifiable Feedback

  • Provide clear feedback for user interactions (e.g. confirmation or error messages)

  • Notify users of changes on the page

  • Make instructions easy to identify Follow popular styles for important feedback
