Text First Organization and Design

As a designer, it's essential to recognize that users arrive with expectations on how a webpage is suppose to look or what they want to get out of using it—even before they interact with your content. While creating a unique product is a skill, following the fundamentals of plain-text for organization makes the product easier to understand through practicability, and therefore more accessible to all users, abled or disabled.

The way content is structured, aligned, and visually presented plays a major role in how people experience a page. These are best applied as early into the design process as possible, as if they accessibility is incorporated later on, the entire project may have to be completely reorganized or the reorganization could be so extensive, restarting may be the easier course.

This section outlines key principles of accessibility for organizing and designing your page both visually and structurally (in code)—hierarchy, scannablity navigation, and readability—to create a smoother, more intuitive experience for everyone.

What are Gestalt Principles?

For understanding what makes a design aesthetic, Gestalt Principles are the principles that describe how humans process visual information: grouping elements, recognize patterns and simplify complex images. There are six elements commonly recognized as Gestalt principles, though some may include seven or divide them in up to twelve categories. More detailed information on These principles can be read through Interaction-Design.org.

  • Proximity: Elements that are close together are perceived as related, helping users quickly identify grouped content.

  • Similarity: Items that look alike (in color, shape, size, etc.) are seen as part of the same group, aiding consistency across a design.

  • Figure/Ground: The mind distinguishes between the main object (figure) and the background (ground), which helps highlight important content.

  • Continuity: Users naturally follow lines and paths, so designs with visual flow lead the eye through content smoothly.

  • Closure: Even when parts of a shape or object are missing, the mind fills in the gaps to see a complete form—useful in logos and icons.

  • Common Region: Elements within the same bounded area are perceived as a group, making layout sections clearer and more organized.

Hierarchy, Navigation, Scannability

Navigation Tips and Accessibility Elements

Predicable with Hierarchy

When text is put into your design, where its located on the page and how it's related to other content will significantly change how readers view the importance of the information and what the information could be before reading the words. It's important to generalize expectations on interpretations.

Different elements of text (article headings, body text, etc.) can be emphasize by having different size, color*, contrast, alignment, and/or texture/type. The meaning across these differences is established by consistency and repetition across the page/multiple pages, and the proximity the text element has to other text elements (An article title).

*Its important to note, color alone should not be used to signal information.

Accessibility: Hierarchy in structural elements

Headings are used to create a hierarchy between different sections. Assistive technologies can use them for in-page navigation. Headings rank in importance from <h1> as the most important to <h6> being the least important and follows logic to group sections of by where information begins.

An article may be organized by Page Title <h1> General Topic 1 <h2> Subcategory of topic 1 <h3> General topic 2 <h2> Subcategory of topic 2 <h3>.

Navigation Scannability

As a designer, it's essential to recognize that users arrive with expectations on how a webpage is suppose to look, how they process information on the page, or what they want to get out of using it—even before they interact with your content.

Users typically follow two patterns while scanning through a page, and optimal page layouts consider this in the placement of its text. Note it is influenced by a culture's written language structure.

  • F-Pattern: This is most common for text-heavy designs. Following the shape of the 'F', users start at the top left, move horizontally across the top, and then scan down the left side of the page for headings and subheadings.

  • Z-Pattern: Common for less dense designs: Following the shape of the' Z', users start at the top left, move horizontally across the top, and then scan diagonally to the lower left corner before scanning horizontally across the bottom of the page.

Where text is located on the page signifies its meanings. Text that is located in the navigation bar will be perceived as apart of title, logo, or navigation buttons. The first paragraph of the page may be an introduction, overview, or summary with an outline of what's to come. .

White spaceseparates and organizes different elements on the page and makes your content less overwhelming.

Indentations, group and chunk elements close together, and adjust how the text is aligned for closer proximity to related elements or "Giving the viewer’s eyes a place to rest and a path to travel through the design." (canva vsual hierarchy).

Readable Text

The organization properties for text being readable seems intuitive, but the way it's nuanced for accessibility guidelines and WCAG standards makes it important to learn the guidelines.

  • Sans-Serif font's are recommended for their accessibility, such as Arial and Helvetica,

  • Font size is based of the letter 'I', and is at a minimum to be 16px tall (size 12 font).

  • Maintain space between line that increases readability. It's recommended for line spacing to be 130% percent to 150% of the text size itself (line spacing 1.5).

  • Text has a contrast ratio of 7:1 for normal text and 4.5:1 for large text.

Accessibility: Readable Text in structural elements

WCAG guidelines recommend percentages for sizing text and containers that hold text elements. It's important to design your webpage with this in mind as inserting too late into the creation will require a complete reorganization and rewrite of code. More in depth information on how to properly incorporate these suggestion can be found through W3.org.

  • Using percent for font sizes

  • Using named font sizes

  • Using em units for font sizes

  • Calculating container size and position in a way that scales with text size

  • Using liquid layout for containers

Consistency

As mentioned previously in some of the sections, consistency is the key to making your product, webpage, design, or content more accessibly. Maintaining consistency for organization techniques makes your content more known to the user as they continue to engage with it, it can increase the speed of how you create content by having your own work to reference, and it can create a subtle identity that only applies to you.
