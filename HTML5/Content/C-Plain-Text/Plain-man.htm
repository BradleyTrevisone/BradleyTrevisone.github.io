Writing in Plain Language

Whether you're writing for a website, or you're David Foster Wallace writing novels intended for other writers, writing in plain language is the most effective way on making your ideas accessible for other people.

Plain Language is writing that is clear, concise, and easy for the intended audience to understand the first time they read it. It avoids unnecessary complexity, uses familiar words and sentence structures, and focuses on helping readers find information quickly, understand it easily, and use it effectively.

This section focuses entirely on the content of what is written. Text accessibility relating to design and style is discussed in another section which can be found in the article Text Organization and Layout Design.

Plain language

Plain language may imply to oversimplify your ideas or sentence structure, but in practice it requires meaningful thought, intelligence, and creativity to write simply. And in case that's not convincing enough, Jane Austen's classic Pride and Prejudice is listed at an 8th grade level!

Bellow are guidelines the U.S. Government lists for Plain language for website and non-web writing. More information can be found through PlainLanguage.Gov.

  • Write for average reader and use familiar words. For web pages, aim for an 8th-grade reading level.

  • Organize to serve the readers needs. Structure should follow, heading>introduction>body>conclusion

  • Titles and Headings are descriptive and written for scannability

  • Use active voice, avoiding "to be" verbs when possible. ( 'is,are, be,' Present tense examples)

  • Use short sections and short sentences. Write even Shorter sections when writing for the web than you would on paper.

  • Address reader as "you" and use proper pronouns throughout.

  • Lists and tables to simplify complex material. Use even more lists and tables than you would use on paper.

  • No more than three subordinate levels.

Direct Writing

Be as direct and concise with your writing as possible. Write in the simplest tense possible, use the most direct version of the verb (avoiding hidden verbs), remove excess words, and remove ambiguity (such as using "must" for requirements instead of "shall".
Confusing writing Preferable writing
All applicants shall be required to complete the submission of the necessary documentation prior to the commencement of the review process, which will not be initiated until said documents have been provided in full. Applicants must submit all required documents before the review process begins.

Tone

Writing your content should sound casual and in a conversation style for your intended audience. It's important to keep a neutral and predictable writing style in order to make your work more scannable and not provoke readers because of inconsistencies. Its also important to repeat words and use similar phrasing across your pages to unify keep being predictable and unify your content.

Definitions and Consistent Terminology

When using an acronym or a term the average user may not understand if they're new to the subject matter, add a definition the first time it's mentioned. Example: Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).

Use meaningful and description text for links. Instead of "Click here" use "Download The PDF Guide".

Use consistent Terminology Across action items, such as buttons or label forms. Always use "Log in" instead of alternating between "Log in" and "Sign in".

Conclusion

When creating content, accessibility for writing may seem like the least relevant part of visual design. However, understanding user behavior determines if they ever access your content through key search words or social media marketing, as well as retaining the users that visit your site because of your thoughtful optimization. Most of the time, it's about keeping a user around longer than the initial 10-20 second window while they're deciding on if they want to use your site or not.
